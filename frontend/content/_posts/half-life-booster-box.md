## Day 1 EV Is a Mirage

The hype cycle for a new Pokémon set is predictable. Leaks trickle out. Content creators film early openings. Card prices spike on presale listings with minimal supply. Someone calculates the EV of a booster box, and the number looks incredible — $180, $200, maybe more against a $110 retail price.

The forums light up. "This set is insane value." "Buy as many boxes as you can." "This is the most +EV product in years."

Fast forward four weeks. That $180 EV box? It's now $95. The same product, same pull rates, same cards. The only thing that changed was the market — and it changed fast.

Every sealed product has an EV half-life: the time it takes for the expected value to drop by half from its peak. Understanding this curve is the difference between making informed decisions and chasing a number that was dead before you opened the box.

## The Anatomy of EV Decay

EV isn't a fixed property of a product. It's a function of two inputs: **pull rates** (which stay constant) and **market prices** (which don't). Since pull rates are baked into the print run, EV movement is entirely driven by card price fluctuations. And card prices follow a remarkably consistent lifecycle.

### Phase 1: Pre-Release Hype (EV Peak)

Before a set officially drops, supply is minimal. Early access openings, staff prerelease events, and leaked product create a tiny pool of available singles. Demand is high — collectors want the new chase cards immediately — and supply is almost nonexistent.

Result: card prices are wildly inflated. A chase alt-art might list at $300–$500 based on a handful of sales. Even mid-tier hits carry premium prices because nobody has them yet.

This is when EV peaks. And it's completely artificial.

### Phase 2: Launch Week (The Cliff)

Product hits shelves. Thousands of boxes get ripped open simultaneously. Content creators film their openings, flooding YouTube and TikTok. Within 48–72 hours, the supply of singles on TCGPlayer and eBay multiplies by 10x or more.

Prices respond immediately. That $400 chase card drops to $250. The $50 full-art falls to $30. Mid-range holos that were $15 settle to $8.

Box EV typically drops **25–40%** in the first week alone. For a set that peaked at $180, you're looking at $110–$135 by day seven.

### Phase 3: The Settling (Weeks 2–6)

The initial flood slows but doesn't stop. Boxes continue selling, more singles enter the market, and prices continue their downward grind. This phase is less dramatic than launch week but equally damaging.

By week four, most sets have lost **50–60%** of their peak EV. Our $180 box is now hovering around $80–$95. For many products, this is where EV dips below retail cost — the box officially becomes -EV for openers.

### Phase 4: The Floor (Month 2+)

Prices find a floor. The most desirable chase cards stabilize as buy-side demand catches up with sell-side supply. Bulk cards settle at their true bulk rates ($0.05–$0.15). The EV flatlines.

For most modern sets, the floor EV is **40–50% of the peak**. Barring external catalysts (competitive meta shifts, nostalgia waves, influencer spikes), this is where the product lives for the rest of its life in print.

## Why This Happens: Supply Floods the Zone

The mechanism is pure supply and demand. A modern Pokémon set has a massive print run. Millions of boxes enter the market. Each box contains roughly 36 packs, each pack with multiple cards. The sheer volume of singles that hits the marketplace within the first month is staggering.

Consider the math: if 500,000 booster boxes of a set are opened in the first month (a conservative estimate for a popular set), and each box has a 1-in-300 chance of containing the top chase card, that's roughly **1,667 copies** of that chase card flooding the market. Pre-release, there might have been 50 copies available. The supply increase is 33x.

No price survives a 33x supply shock.

## The Sell Window: When Timing Beats EV

If you're opening product to sell singles — not just for the fun of ripping packs — timing matters more than EV. There's a narrow window, typically **24–72 hours after you get the product**, where card prices are still elevated enough to make opening profitable.

This is the sell window. And it's brutally short.

The collectors who profit from sealed product aren't the ones who open boxes on a Saturday afternoon and list cards the following weekend. They're the ones who open at midnight on release day, photograph and list cards before sunrise, and ship by noon. They're treating it as a time-sensitive arbitrage, not a casual hobby.

For everyone else — the people who open a box, admire their pulls, maybe sleeve the good ones, and get around to selling in a week or two — the EV has already decayed past the break-even point.

## The Bulk Problem Nobody Talks About

Here's a dirty secret of EV calculations: most include bulk cards at their theoretical market price. A common card "worth" $0.05 times 200+ commons in a box adds $10–$15 to the EV.

In theory.

In practice, nobody is selling individual commons for $0.05. The transaction cost (listing, packaging, shipping) far exceeds the card's value. Your real options for bulk are:

- **Sell to a local card shop at buylist prices** — typically $3–$5 per 1,000 cards, giving you maybe $1–$2 for your box's bulk.
- **List bulk lots on eBay** — slightly better, but you're still looking at $5–$8 after fees for a box's worth of bulk and shipping.
- **Keep them in a box in your closet** — the most common outcome, and worth exactly $0.

Honest EV calculations should use realistic buylist prices for bulk, not theoretical retail prices. When you make that adjustment, most box EVs drop another $8–$12. That "+EV" product just got a lot closer to break-even.

## Print Runs: The Hidden Variable

Not all EV decay curves are equal. The key differentiator is print run size.

**Unlimited print run sets** (most modern main series sets) have the steepest decay curves. The Pokémon Company can and will print more product as long as demand exists. This means supply continuously enters the market, applying constant downward pressure on card prices.

**Limited print run sets** (special sets, holiday collections, Japanese exclusives) decay more slowly and often find higher floors. When the product is genuinely scarce, the supply flood is contained. Card prices may still drop from pre-release peaks, but they stabilize at higher levels because no new product is entering the market.

This creates an interesting dynamic: limited print run products with lower initial EV can actually be better long-term value propositions than unlimited products with sky-high launch EV. The limited product's EV might only drop 20–30%, while the unlimited product craters 60%.

## What This Means for Your Decisions

Understanding the EV half-life changes how you evaluate sealed product:

**1. Never trust pre-release EV.** It's the peak of an artificial bubble. If someone tells you a box has $200 EV before street date, your response should be "for how long?"

**2. Look at the EV decay curve, not a snapshot.** A product's value trajectory tells you more than its current EV. Is it early in the decay (still dropping fast) or has it found its floor (prices stabilized)?

**3. If you're opening to sell, respect the sell window.** Have your listing workflow ready before you open the box. Delays cost real money.

**4. For sealed holding, buy after the floor.** If you want to hold sealed product as an investment, the best entry point is after the EV decay has run its course — typically 2–3 months post-release. You're buying at the bottom, not the top.

**5. Compare EV floors across sets, not EV peaks.** The set with the "best EV" at launch might have the worst EV at month three. The floor is what matters for long-term product evaluation.
