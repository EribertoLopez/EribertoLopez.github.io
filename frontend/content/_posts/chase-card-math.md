## Everyone Wants the Charizard

Open any Pokémon card community — Reddit, YouTube, Discord — and the conversation gravitates to the same place: the chase card. The Charizard alt-art. The Pikachu gold star. The illustration rare that makes people lose their minds on camera.

These cards are the headliners. They drive sealed product sales, dominate thumbnail clickbait, and define how collectors judge an entire set. "Is the set worth it?" really means "Does it have a chase card I want?"

But here's the question nobody asks: **What does that chase card actually contribute to your box?**

Let's do the math. And the math is uncomfortable.

## $500 × 0.33% = $1.67

A typical ultra-premium chase card — let's say a Charizard illustration rare — sells for around $500 on the secondary market. That's real. You can look it up on TCGPlayer right now.

The pull rate for that card is roughly 1 in 300 packs. A booster box has 36 packs. So your odds of pulling it from a single booster box are approximately:

**36 / 300 = 12%, or about 1 in 8.3 boxes**

More precisely, using the per-pack pull rate: each pack has a 0.33% chance. Across 36 packs in a box, your cumulative probability is roughly 11.3% (accounting for independent events).

Now multiply the probability by the price:

**$500 × 0.113 = $56.50 contribution to box EV**

Wait — that's not $1.67. You're right. The $1.67 figure is per *pack*, not per box. But even at the box level, let's put that $56.50 in context.

If the box's total EV is $130, that single chase card accounts for **43% of the entire EV**. One card. Out of hundreds of possible pulls. Nearly half the expected value of the product rests on a card you have an 89% chance of *not* pulling.

That's not a value proposition. That's a lottery ticket wearing a trench coat.

## The EV Before and After

Here's where it gets really interesting. Strip the chase card from the EV calculation entirely:

- **Full EV (with chase):** $130
- **EV without top chase card:** $73.50

That $130 EV box? Without the lottery ticket, it's worth $73.50 — against a retail price of $110. That's not +EV. That's **-$36.50** for the ~89% of openers who don't hit the chase.

Now extend this to the top three chase cards in a set. A typical modern Pokémon set has 2–3 ultra-premium pulls: the marquee Charizard, a Pikachu variant, and maybe a trainer gallery secret rare. Together, they might account for $80–$90 of a box's $130 EV.

Remove them, and the "base" EV — the value of everything else combined — is $40–$50. Less than half the retail price of the box.

**The chase cards aren't supporting the EV. They *are* the EV.** Everything else is window dressing.

## Why Your Brain Gets This Wrong

Humans are terrible at evaluating low-probability, high-reward scenarios. Behavioral economists call this **probability weighting** — we systematically overestimate the likelihood of rare events and underestimate common ones.

When you see a $500 Charizard get pulled on a YouTube video, your brain doesn't process "0.33% per pack." It processes "that could be me." The emotional weight of the outcome ($500! From a $4 pack!) overwhelms the statistical reality (you'd need to open ~300 packs, or about 8.3 boxes at $110 each — $913 worth of product — to have a coin-flip chance).

This is the same cognitive bias that drives lottery ticket sales. The jackpot is real. The odds are irrelevant to the feeling. And content creators — who film hundreds of openings and only upload the hits — amplify this distortion to an extreme.

You're not watching a representative sample. You're watching a highlight reel. And your brain is pricing the box based on the highlight reel.

## The Poker Player's Perspective

In poker, this concept is well understood. A player who goes all-in with a 10% chance to win a $1,000 pot is making a reasonable play — *if the cost to enter is less than $100*. The expected value is clear: 10% × $1,000 = $100 EV.

But a poker player would never confuse "positive EV" with "I will probably win this hand." They understand that 90% of the time, they lose their buy-in. They're profitable over hundreds of hands because the math works *in aggregate*.

Pokémon box openings work the same way. The EV math works in aggregate — across thousands of boxes. But you're not a casino running thousands of boxes through a machine. You're one person opening one box. And for that one box, the chase card contribution to EV is a phantom. It's real in expectation, imaginary in experience.

The poker player who goes all-in 10 times expecting to win each time goes broke. The collector who opens 10 boxes expecting to hit the chase goes broker.

## Decomposing the Box: Where Value Actually Lives

Let's break down where box EV actually comes from, using a typical modern set:

**Top chase cards (1–3 cards, <1% pull rate each):**
- Contribution to box EV: $50–$90 (40–70% of total EV)
- Probability of pulling at least one: 15–25% per box
- Reality: Most openers never see these.

**Mid-tier hits (full arts, V/VMAX/ex, 10–20 cards, 2–5% pull rate each):**
- Contribution to box EV: $25–$40 (20–30% of total EV)
- Probability of pulling several: ~95% per box
- Reality: This is where your actual returns come from. The $15–$30 full arts, the $8–$12 ex cards. Not sexy. Not clickbait. But real.

**Holos and reverse holos (50+ cards, moderate pull rates):**
- Contribution to box EV: $10–$15 (8–12% of total EV)
- Reality: Mostly $1–$3 cards. Liquid enough to sell but not exciting.

**Bulk commons, uncommons, and energy:**
- Contribution to box EV: $5–$10 (theoretical)
- Reality: Worth $1–$3 at buylist. Practically unsellable individually.

The takeaway? **Mid-tier hits are the real value engine.** They're the on-base percentage to the chase card's home run. Less glamorous, more reliable, and ultimately what determines whether your box opening was a success or a loss.

## The "Chase Premium": What You're Really Paying For

Here's a framework that makes the economics clearer. Think of a booster box price as two components:

**Base value:** The expected return from all non-chase pulls. For most sets, this is $50–$70.

**Chase premium:** The difference between retail price and base value. If a box costs $110 and the base value is $60, you're paying a $50 "chase premium" — essentially a lottery ticket bundled into the product.

Is that lottery ticket worth $50? At 15–25% odds of hitting a $200–$500 card, the EV of the lottery ticket is $30–$125 depending on the set. Sometimes it's a fair price. Sometimes it's a terrible deal. But either way, you should *know* you're buying a lottery ticket.

Most collectors don't frame it this way. They look at the full EV ($130) vs. the price ($110) and see a $20 profit opportunity. In reality, they're paying $50 for a lottery ticket with a 75–85% chance of returning $0.
